Welcome to WeSay 0.7.x-Development

You have installed a development version of WeSay.  This is a team effort, and we really appreciate your willingness to give WeSay a spin and send us feedback. 

About this version

WeSay comes in two flavors: “stable” (even numbered ones) and “development” (odd numbered ones).   The even versions receive critical bug fixes, but not new features or anything risky.

If crashes or other bugs would be a major problem for you, please uninstall this version and get our most recent 0.6.xxxx one.  If you are helping us advance WeSay, are evaluating WeSay’s capabilities, or are just willing to risk the occasional bug, this is the version for you.  This is where we are currently adding new abilities in response to your requests, and we need people to help ensure that it can become stable, too. Eventually, we will retire 0.6 and 0.7 will be relabeled “0.8 - Stable”. Note that at times we post new “odd” versions almost daily.  If you report a bug via issues@wesay.org, we can often times fix it and provide a new release within hours.  So let us know if you’re in a hurry for a fix.

What’s New in 0.7?

Word Packs

Previously, WeSay offered a task for eliciting simple word lists.  As you entered a word, WeSay made a dictionary entry of it and used the elicitation word for the definition.  Now, with Word Packs, that word list can be an entire template dictionary.  For example, with the included “SIL Comparative African Wordlist” Word Pack, when you enter a word, the newly created entry gets a part of speech, semantic domains, glosses in several languages, and a reference to the SIL-CAWL item.  Additionally, since Word Packs are multilingual, you can elicit words from SIL-CAWL in English, French, Hausa, Portuguese, Swahili.

Team Collaboration using Send/Receive & Chorus

WeSay promotes teamwork beyond what people normally do with dictionary applications. Starting with WeSay 0.7,  multiple team members in the same office or around the world can work on the same dictionary, whether they are connected to the internet always, occasionally, or never.  If two people edit the same thing at the same time, WeSay does its best to merge their work; if it can’t automatically deal with a conflict, it just chooses one person’s work, makes a note of what happened, and then allows any member of the team, anywhere, to see what happened and fix up the entry if needed.  WeSay does this using Chorus, a software toolbox we’ve created to support collaboration in language development projects. You’ll see elements of Chorus showing up in other language software applications like FieldWorks, Paratext, OurWord, and OneStory Editor.  Using the new Chorus-enabled WeSay, you can:

Get automatic incremental backups of your work which would allow reversing back to any previous stage (would require technical help).

Send your changes to an internet server, USB key, or local server, and receive changes from your colleagues.

See a history of who has done what to the dictionary.

Leave questions for teammates, who can answer them. When everything is ok, you can mark the issue as “resolved”.

 

What Now?

Get your head around WeSay… it’s really different.  It’s aimed at empowering a very different user than you are, and approaches the task of dictionary making in a new, super-task-oriented way.  Using the provided samples, play around with both WeSay and the WeSay Configuration Tool.  To do this, go to your Windows Start menu and find “WeSay”.  Choose one of the icons there which open the sample project.
